# Resume & Cover Letter Writing Style Rules

Apply these rules to every piece of job-application prose produced in this project: resume bullets, cover letter paragraphs, summaries, and any LinkedIn-style bios. When in doubt, state the point directly without rhetorical setup.

---

## Forbidden Patterns (AI Tells)

### The Correlative Construction — #1 AI Tell

Never use:
- "X aren't just Y - they're Z"
- "X isn't just about Y, it's about Z"
- "It's not about X, it's about Y"

State the point directly instead.

### Forbidden Constructions

- "The truth is..." / "The reality is..."
- "Let that sink in"
- "Now more than ever..."
- "The best part? ..."
- "The secret? ..."
- "Sounds impossible? It's not."
- "What if I told you..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Let's be honest..."
- "At the end of the day..."

### Forbidden Staccato Phrasing (faux-dramatic fragments)

- "No fluff. No filler. Just results."
- "Big ideas. Small words. Maximum impact."
- "Stop guessing. Start winning."

### Forbidden Openers

- "In the ever-evolving world of..."
- "In today's fast-paced..."
- "Gone are the days when..."
- "This underscores..."
- "In an era where..."

---

## Formatting Restrictions

- No boldface for emphasis in body text
- No emojis unless explicitly requested
- No "In conclusion" / "In summary" closers
- No bullet points unless explicitly asked
- No em dashes (—) or en dashes (–) — use a regular hyphen with spaces - like this - instead

---

## Anti-AI Pattern Detection

### 1. Overuse of "Just"

Remove "just" when used mid-sentence as a softener or more than once per paragraph.
Acceptable: "just one question," "just in case," "just started."

### 2. Overuse of "Actually"

Limit to once per 500 words. Only acceptable when correcting a prior misunderstanding.

### 3. Hedging Language

Remove hedge words unless genuine uncertainty exists. If you believe the statement, state it plainly.

- "might help" -> "will help"
- "Some people say..." -> "I've found that..."
- "It could be argued that..." -> "It's true that..."
- "Perhaps we should consider..." -> "We should..."
- "One could say..." -> state the claim directly

Audit for: might, could, perhaps, possibly, maybe, somewhat, "it seems like."

### 4. Passive Voice

Identify who is doing the action and rewrite with a subject.

- "It was determined that improvements were needed" -> "We need to improve"
- "It has been shown that users benefit from..." -> "Users benefit from..."

### 5. Corporate Jargon

Rewrite plainly - would you say this to a friend?

- "Leveraging synergies across stakeholder ecosystems" -> "Getting different people talking to each other"
- "Optimize engagement metrics" -> "Get people paying attention"

### 6. Vague Language

Back every claim with a specific number, example, or name.

- "Our users improve" -> "Users improve by 40% in 6 months"
- "Many people think..." -> "A survey found that 73% of users..."
- "Significant improvements" -> "Improvements of 23-47%"

### 7. Too Many Transition Words

Limit transitions to: but, and, so, then, because.
Avoid: furthermore, moreover, in addition to this, it is important to note that.

### 8. Overcomplicated Sentence Structure

Break long complex sentences into short plain ones.

- "The multifaceted nature of contemporary paradigms... necessitates a comprehensive reevaluation of traditional methodologies." -> "Things have changed. We need to rethink our approach."
